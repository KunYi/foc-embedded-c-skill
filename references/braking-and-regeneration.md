# Braking and Regeneration (Reference)

## Overview
Under acceleration, the inverter delivers electrical power from the DC bus to the motor. Under deceleration, backdriving, load overhauling, or commanded negative torque, the same power stage can reverse the energy flow: the motor behaves as a generator and the drive becomes a controlled interface that pushes energy back toward the DC bus.

This is why a motor drive can effectively behave like an inverter/energy source interface during braking. The firmware must not treat negative `Iq` as "just another command" without asking where the returned energy will go.

## 1. Energy-Flow Modes

### A. Coasting
- PWM torque production is removed and the motor freewheels.
- Little active braking torque is generated by the controller.
- Risk: at high speed, BEMF can still charge the DC bus through body-diode or synchronous conduction paths depending on the hardware state.

### B. Controlled Regenerative Braking
- The controller commands negative torque so the machine converts mechanical energy into electrical energy.
- If the DC source can absorb energy, this is efficient and desirable.
- Examples: battery systems, active front ends, four-quadrant supplies, grid-tied regenerative stages.

### C. Dissipative Dynamic Braking
- Mechanical energy is intentionally burned as heat rather than returned upstream.
- Typical path: brake chopper plus resistor across the DC bus.
- Use when the DC source cannot safely sink regenerated current or when bus-voltage rise must be clamped locally.

### D. Emergency Braking / ASC
- Used for abnormal or unsafe states, not as the default comfort-braking strategy.
- Energy is dumped mainly into the motor winding and switching path losses, with strong braking torque and thermal stress.
- Use when high speed or uncontrolled regeneration makes normal coasting or ordinary regen unsafe.

## 2. The Critical Question: Can the DC Bus Absorb Energy?

Always determine the bus-energy sink capability before designing braking behavior.

### A. Battery or Other Absorbing Source
- A battery-backed bus can often absorb regenerative energy, but only within charge-current, voltage, temperature, and BMS limits.
- Regeneration may need derating when the battery is full, cold, disconnected, or current-limited.

### B. Active Front End / Four-Quadrant Supply
- Some industrial supplies can sink power back to mains or an intermediate energy buffer.
- Verify the actual sink rating, dynamic response, and overvoltage thresholds instead of assuming all "programmable" supplies can absorb energy.

### C. Bench PSU or Simple AC-DC Supply
- Many lab and commodity power supplies source current well but cannot safely sink returned energy.
- In this case, regenerative braking will raise `Vbus`, often very quickly.
- The drive must then reduce negative torque, activate a brake chopper, switch braking mode, or trip to a safe state.

### D. Bus Capacitor Only
- Bus capacitance can absorb only transient energy.
- This is a short-term buffer, not a true energy sink.
- High inertia or repeated deceleration events will still drive the bus into OVP unless another energy path exists.

## 3. Braking Strategy Selection

Choose braking behavior from both product intent and bus capability:

- **Normal commanded deceleration**:
  - prefer controlled regen if the bus can absorb it
  - otherwise use limited decel plus dissipative braking if available
- **Precision speed reduction**:
  - prefer closed-loop negative torque with explicit bus-voltage supervision
- **High-inertia stop with weak bus sink**:
  - likely requires brake chopper or a decel profile constrained by bus-energy limits
- **Emergency halt**:
  - choose between High-Z, controlled braking collapse, or ASC based on overspeed risk, bus survivability, and mechanical survivability

Do not conflate `normal brake` and `emergency halt`. They optimize for different outcomes.

## 4. Firmware Rules

- A negative `Iq` command is only valid if the bus-energy path is known and safe.
- Bus overvoltage supervision must remain active during every braking mode.
- Brake commands should be limited by both machine current capability and DC-bus absorption capability.
- If the source cannot sink power, the deceleration planner must reduce allowable negative torque or redirect energy into a dissipative path.
- If the system claims regenerative braking, the firmware should know when regen is unavailable and fall back predictably.

## 5. What Engineers Must Be Told Explicitly

When AI proposes braking logic, it should say all of the following explicitly when relevant:

- the motor-drive bridge can reverse power flow during deceleration
- the DC bus is not an infinite sink
- a drive attached to a non-sinking supply can overvoltage the bus during negative torque
- brake resistor systems are dissipative, not regenerative
- ASC is an emergency containment method, not a general-purpose energy-recovery strategy

## 6. Acceptance Criteria

- **Bus-voltage control**: during the worst commanded deceleration, `Vbus` stays below the defined warning and fault limits with margin.
- **Sink-path validation**: if regen is claimed, measure and confirm that the upstream source actually absorbs current instead of only letting `Vbus` rise.
- **Brake-chopper validation**: verify chopper turn-on/off thresholds, duty behavior, resistor temperature rise, and MOSFET stress under repeated decel events.
- **Mode distinction**: commanded braking, fault braking, and emergency halt produce the documented and distinct behavior.
- **Fallback behavior**: when regen becomes unavailable, the firmware transitions to the documented reduced-decel, dissipative, or fault strategy without uncontrolled bus overvoltage.
- **High-inertia validation**: test with representative inertia or equivalent energy storage, not only unloaded motors.

## 7. Manual Verification Plan

- Log `Vbus`, `Iq_ref`, measured speed, braking mode, fault/warning flags, and brake-chopper state during acceleration and deceleration sweeps.
- Probe brake-chopper gate, bus voltage, and supply current direction together when validating dissipative versus regenerative behavior.
- On battery or active-front-end systems, verify negative bus current or equivalent sink telemetry during controlled regeneration.
- On bench-supply systems, deliberately command negative torque and verify the firmware limits or redirects energy before bus overvoltage.
- Validate repeated braking events at cold and hot hardware temperature, full and reduced bus voltage, and worst-case inertia.
